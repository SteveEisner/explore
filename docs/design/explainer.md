## Content summary
This page explains how a soccer ball is made and what it’s made of. It introduces the ball as three nested layers — bladder, lining, and cover — then walks through the build stages: materials, cutting panels, joining them, inserting the bladder, and testing.

Along the way it highlights practical trade-offs, like stitching methods and bladder materials, and why they affect durability, feel, and upkeep. A legend keeps the visuals consistent, a glossary defines recurring terms, and a short historical note explains the classic black-and-white look.

The piece ends by clarifying scope: it’s a general explainer meant to build intuition, not a step-by-step manufacturing manual.

## Design notes
This explainer is built to teach by layering: a big-picture hero, a consistent visual language, then a step-by-step flow that mirrors how the object is actually made. The structure stays neutral, while custom HTML and CSS carry the personality, so content and presentation can evolve without breaking the layout.

The palette is intentionally rooted in the subject. Pitch greens and chalk white suggest the field, leather black signals the cover, and a bright flash color highlights state, selections, and key callouts. That makes the drawings feel coherent and helps the legend do real work instead of being decorative.

Typography is split into display and body roles: condensed, uppercase headings for punch and hierarchy, and a calmer body face for reading. A monospace accent is used sparingly for labels and small spec-like details, reinforcing the “guide” vibe.

The hero sets expectations in one glance: what the thing is, what the reader will learn, and a quick set of real-world specs to anchor it. The small animated spin adds life, but motion is minimal and respects reduced-motion settings so it doesn’t become a distraction.

The legend and glossary are anchors. The legend establishes a color key for bladder, lining, and cover that repeats across the SVG diagrams, so once the reader learns it, they can decode every stage quickly. The glossary captures recurring terms and keeps the language consistent.

The stage gallery is the main navigation because this is a process story. Each stage pairs a diagram with short prose and a “why” note, so readers get both the mechanical step and the reason it matters. Options like stitching methods and bladder materials are included because they explain real trade-offs a reader will care about: price, durability, feel, and maintenance.

The fun fact and footer are there to round it out. The fun fact connects the iconic black-and-white look to television history, which explains why that detail exists. The footer sets boundaries: this is a general-audience explainer, not a manufacturing manual, so we deliberately omit industrial machinery, chemistry, and production specifics.
